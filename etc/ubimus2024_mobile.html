Development and evaluation of an improvising machine in an ubimus context

Felippe Barros (Master of Arts-UFES) and Leandro Costalonga (Electronics and Computing Department-UFES)

       

arrow_forward 

This paper points to connections between the development of improvising machines for free improvisation and the ideological background of ubimus practices. To this end, we developed an open-source Pure Data system named “engenhoca”. The system was evaluated with a non-experimental approach, by collecting quantitative and qualitative data from three experienced musicians in the genre. Aspects of the evaluation will be discussed concerning the system and its relation to the improvisers’ expectations in a genre where explicit opinions are usually refrained from being expressed.

Introduction

  • Ubiquitous music: intersection of musical creation, ubiquitous computing tools, concerns related to the context and social interaction of participants [Pimenta et al. 2009]
  • Some iniatives have been related to free improvisation [Stolfi et al. 2018] and [Clemente et al. 2020]
  • Anti-formalist approach to music-making, abstaining from explicit use of conventional musical structures and techniques in favor of sonic exploration and interplay between musicians

  • Fertile ground for the use of musical agents
  • Class of interactive systems that demonstrate a sense of independence and agency when interacting musically
  • Improvising machines: one of the categories suggested by Tatar and Pasquier (2019)
  • Connections between the development of improvising machines and ubimus

  • FLOSS improvising machine for free improvisation contexts developed in Pure Data
  • Avaiable at: https://github.com/ofefo/engenhoca
  • Evaluation: non-experimental method involving three experienced musicians, focusing on both quantitative and qualitative feedback
  • Our findings offer insights into the expectations of improvisers, often undisclosed when dealing with human partners

The system

  • Hear what was played, segmenting into phrases
  • Classify sound events into meaningful musical data, storying it in long-term memory
  • Look for local changes in musical texture, deriving decisions about what to do next
  • Sonic responses as synthesized or MIDI output

  • Clustering with K-means algorithm
  • Timbre: (0) non-percussive and (1) percussive sound
  • Frequency: (0) low, (1) middle and (2) high registers
  • Intensity: (0) piano, (1) mezzo and (2) forte
  • Durations: (0) long, (1) moderate and (2) short notes

  • Phrases for each sound parameter are recorded in distinct databases
  • Each phrase is matched into a database according to the statistical mode for parameter values
  • Past and current improvisation sessions

  • Change in musical texture triggers a change in behavior
  • First order-markov chain: approximate playing patterns
  • Example: slow passages are followed by fast paced ones
  • Phrase selected for the parameter can be manipulated by transformative mathematical operations

  • Audio synthesis is done by FM synthesizer
  • Five sound effects can modify the synthesizer ouput
  • MIDI output is possible

  • Real-time, low latency network interaction
  • Two instances of engenhoca
  • Quacktrip: a Pure Data implementation of the JackTrip audio transmission tool

Evaluation of engenhoca

  • Three volunteers, with experience in free improvisation practices
  • Three improvisation sessions and a set of questions after each one
  • Five-alternative Linkert scale
  • Inspired by Hsu and Sosnick (2009)
  • Qualitative evaluation with one paragraph impressions

  • Satisfied with timbre, rhythmic and melodic aspects; Strong satisfaction with long-term responsiveness
  • Worst evaluation: short-term responsiveness
  • Musicians made adjustments to the way the machine played
  • Greater dispersion of results for short and long-term responsiveness
  • Musicans said to modify their interaction approach after each session

  • Struggle to access numerous experienced improvisers
  • Participation of inexperienced musicians could allow for more data, possibly providing statistically significant results, but might be difficult for a broader population of musicians that are not familiar with free improvisation
  • Opinions expressed in this evaluation will only be understood as initial hypothesis to be verified in future work

  • Lower SD in timbral, melodic and rhythmic aspects
  • Larger SD in responsiveness evlatuations
  • To evaluate interaction aspects might be more difficult than delimited musical materials

  • Banerji (2018): musicians suggested learning capabilities in systems without such functionalities
  • Musicians might be the ones adapting to machine behavior
  • Zajonc (1968): exposure effect could explain changes in perception

  • Last improvisation session had greatest satisfaction
  • Increase in evaluation metrics did not occur linearly
  • Musicians expressed a tendency to adapt their playing, in line with Banerji (2018)

  • Banerji (2012): evaluations might reveal "aesthetic preferences, but more crucially, their expectations and understandings of improvisation as social interaction"
  • One musician observed better responses to staccato textures
  • Unlike other parameters, articulation was directly replicated by the machine
  • One musician expressed the system did not behave like a human, while other thought it did
  • To "control" the system seemed to be fruitless, but was tried by one of the musicians. An interaction model that might differ from those experienced with humans

Discussion

  • Satisfaction with timbres and long-term responsiveness
  • Dissatisfaction with short-term changes
  • Suggested good potential for the system to facilitate the discovery of new satisfying musical combinations

  • To improvise with machines is distinct than to improvise with humans
  • Do we want it to be equal?
  • Quantitatively assess complex musical interactions might be more difficult than to evaluate aesthetic materials
  • Novel evaluations could be done with larger pools of participants, with the use of control groups and investigating whether ubimus theoretical approach makes sense for improvisers

  • Engenhoca could be a potential tool for various artistic endeavors
  • Designed to be acessible and adaptable
  • Network connectivity can bring a broader scope for musical interactions
  • FLOSS status enhances its potential for widespread improvements and modifications
